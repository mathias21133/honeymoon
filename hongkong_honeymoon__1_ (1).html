Base — Island Shangri-La

Island Shangri-La · Supreme Court Rd, Admiralty · HK Island

Location + Why It's Right

Admiralty is one of the best possible bases on HK Island — directly above Admiralty MTR, between Central and Wan Chai, with Pacific Place shopping and dining at your feet. Everything on HK Island is close: Bakehouse (Wan Chai) is 10 min walk, Halfway Coffee (Sheung Wan) is 2 MTR stops, Bar Leone and Tai Kwun (Central) are 5 min by MTR or a pleasant 15-min walk downhill. For the Kowloon days — Temple Street, Mong Kok markets, Moonzen Brewery — you cross under the harbor on the MTR (10–15 min) or take the Star Ferry from Central Pier (7 min, HK$3.40). The hotel sits above the park with harbor views from upper floors and feels removed from the tourist scrum of TST while keeping you connected to everything.

Coffee Note

Halfway Coffee (your top specialty pick) is 2 MTR stops in Sheung Wan — 10 min door to door. Bakehouse egg tarts are a 10-min walk through Wan Chai. 18 Grams has a Central branch that's essentially walkable. Lan Fong Yuen (original, 2 Gage St) is 10 min by MTR or 20-min walk through the park. Knockbox is one stop east in Wan Chai. You're well positioned for every coffee and morning stop on this trip — no ferry needed to start most days.

Getting Around

Octopus card (pick up at any MTR station on arrival) handles all transit. Admiralty MTR sits directly below the hotel — Island Line west to Central/Sheung Wan, east to Wan Chai/Causeway Bay. For Kowloon days: MTR through the harbor tunnel (10–15 min to Jordan/Mong Kok) or Star Ferry from Central Pier (7 min, HK$3.40) — take the ferry whenever time allows. Taxis are cheap — most HK Island cross-town trips HK$40–60. Airport Express from Hong Kong Station (Central, 2 stops west, 5 min) — 24 min to HKG.

Hollywood Road and Cat Street (Upper Lascar Row) are the antique district. Porcelain, jade, old maps, colonial-era curios. No need to buy anything — just wander. Lunch · Stall / Street
Wan Chai Market Stalls — Graze ★
The Wan Chai wet market area spills out onto the surrounding streets with cooked-food stalls. This is where local HK workers eat lunch — cheap, fast, Cantonese. Get BBQ pork on rice (char siu fan), wonton noodle soup, or a clay pot rice if they're running one. No menu, point and order. Exactly the kind of eating you want more of.
Wan Chai Market area, around Queen's Rd East + Spring Garden Ln · Cash only · ~HK$50–80/person
🏛2:00pm
Architecture · Neighbourhood
PMQ + Central Mid-Levels Escalator
PMQ is a converted colonial police married quarters — now independent design studios, concept stores, and cafes. Worth 30–45 min. Then hop on the Central–Mid-Levels Escalator — the longest outdoor covered escalator in the world, stretching 800m uphill through Soho. Rides uphill after 10am, downhill until 10am. The streets around it (Elgin, Shelley, Staunton) are dense with cafes, hole-in-the-wall restaurants, and some of the best people-watching in HK.
PMQ: 35 Aberdeen St, Central · Escalator: Central to Mid-Levels, runs 10:30am–midnight uphill
🚶3:30pm
No Agenda · Just Walk
Tai Ping Shan Street — Unstructured Wander ★
Put the phone away. Walk back toward Sheung Wan from PMQ and find Tai Ping Shan Street — one of the oldest streets in HK, lined with incense shops, small ancestral temples with offerings burning out front, dried seafood merchants with sacks of scallops and abalone spilling onto the pavement, paper goods shops making ritual objects. It smells like sandalwood and brine. Walk slowly. Turn down whatever alleyways look interesting. The wet market lanes off Possession Street are worth finding. Buy something from whoever is selling it. This is the Sheung Wan that exists between the specialty coffee shops — it's been here for 150 years and is still completely itself.
Tai Ping Shan St, Sheung Wan · No map needed · Walk south from Hollywood Rd or west from PMQ · Just wander

🦪7:00pm
Dinner · Street · No Reservation
Wan Chai Typhoon Shelter Seafood or Cooked Food Stalls ★
You've spent the day in Wan Chai and Sheung Wan — stay in the neighborhood for dinner. Two options: the typhoon shelter seafood boats in Causeway Bay (floating dai pai dong style, fishermen cook fresh seafood on the water — crab, clams, wok-fried with black bean and chili) or the cooked food stalls around the Wan Chai Market area and Johnston Rd (BBQ pork on rice, wonton noodle, clay pot, roast duck). Either way you're eating on the street or at a plastic table, it costs next to nothing, and it's exactly the right follow to a day built around neighborhood wandering. Save the fine dining for later in the trip when you've earned it.
Typhoon shelter: Causeway Bay typhoon shelter, off Gloucester Rd · Cooked food stalls: Johnston Rd / Wan Chai Market area · Cash only · ~HK$150–300/person
The typhoon shelter boats are the more unique experience — ask your hotel concierge to point you to the current operators. The stalls off Johnston Rd are always running and need no guidance.
🚃9:30pm
Transit · Do Not Cab This
Ding Ding Tram Home — Upper Deck ★
Do not take a cab back to the hotel. Catch the Ding Ding tram on Johnston Rd or Hennessy Rd — HK$3, upper deck, front seat if you can get it. The double-decker rattles west through Wan Chai and Admiralty, close enough to the neon signs to touch them, past open shop fronts and wet markets shutting down for the night. Ride it to Admiralty or Central and walk up from there. 20 minutes. This is one of the best things you can do in HK and it requires zero planning — just don't call a Grab.
Any tram stop on Johnston Rd or Hennessy Rd, Wan Chai · Westbound toward Kennedy Town · HK$3 · Upper deck front seat

Breakfast · Optional · Push-Cart Dim Sum
Lin Heung Kui — Traditional Dim Sum Hall Optional ★★ Disappearing
The last traditional push-cart dim sum hall in Hong Kong. 7:30am opening, immediately packed with locals — families, retirees, people who've been coming here every Saturday morning for decades. Women push steel trolleys through the room calling out what's on them in Cantonese; you flag them down, they stamp your card. No English menu, no pictures, just point at the bamboo steamers going past. Har gow, siu mai, cheung fun, lo mai gai, egg tarts from the trolley. It's loud, chaotic, and completely alive. This experience is dying — the push-cart tradition is nearly gone in HK. If you can get up for it, do it. If not, cha chaan teng is the backup and is equally good in its own way.
160–164 Wellington St, Sheung Wan · MTR: Sheung Wan · Opens 7:30am Sat · Cash only · Arrive at opening — fills within 20 min · ~HK$100–150/person
Optional but recommended. If you go: arrive at 7:30am, find a table, wait for the trolleys to come to you. Don't overthink the ordering — just point at whatever looks good as it passes. You can't really make a wrong choice.

Market · Walk
Mong Kok — Flower Market + Bird Garden ★
The Flower Market on Flower Market Road is one of the great sensory experiences in HK — hundreds of stalls, whole orchids, tropical blooms, buckets of cut flowers, locals buying for shrines and homes. Walk it end to end. Two minutes away, the Bird Garden is a covered arcade of twittering songbirds in ornate bamboo cages and old men playing cards. This is old HK — not a tourist attraction, just life. Budget 45–60 min total.

Market · Wander
Ladies Market — Tung Choi St ★
Stretches 10+ blocks through Mong Kok — clothes, knock-off bags, electronics, toys, street food carts weaving through everything. Not a shopping destination — an experience. The density and chaos of it is the point. Snack as you walk: egg waffles (gai daan jai) from any cart, skewers, fried tofu with hoisin. This is the street-eating HK you came for.
Tung Choi St, Mong Kok · Open from ~noon but 10am stalls are already setting up · Cash for everything

Yat Lok Roast Goose Michelin Star ★★ Must
Michelin-starred roast goose — cash only, no frills, communal tables, you might queue 15 min. The goose is the reason: crackling lacquered skin, intensely savory, served over rice with the pan drippings. This is the single most essential HK dish you can eat on this trip. Museum
M+ Museum ★
Asia's leading contemporary visual culture museum — opened 2021, world-class building on the West Kowloon waterfront. The collection is genuinely excellent: design, architecture, moving image, and visual art from Hong Kong, China, and Asia. The building itself by Herzog & de Meuron is worth seeing. Budget 2 hours minimum. The harbor-facing facade view from inside is one of the best architectural moments in HK.
38 Museum Dr, West Kowloon · HK$120 · MTR: West Kowloon · Opens 10am · Allow 2 hrs

Evening — The Night Market
🔮7:30pm
Night Market · Signature Experience
Temple Street Night Market ★★
One of the great night markets in Asia — not just a shopping arcade, a full neighborhood experience. Enter from Jordan MTR end. The first section is stalls: watches, jade, clothing, souvenirs. Then it opens into the outdoor dai pai dong (cooked food stalls) section in the middle — plastic stools on the street, steaming clay pots, fresh seafood in tanks, locals eating shoulder-to-shoulder. End of the market: Cantonese opera performers, fortune tellers with caged birds (pai kua — the birds pick a card for your fortune), mahjong games spilling out of open doorways. Stay at least 90 minutes. Eat here — it's the meal, not just the snack.
Temple St, Jordan/Yau Ma Tei · MTR: Jordan · Opens dusk, best 8–10pm · Mostly cash · Free to wander
Eat at the outdoor dai pai dong stalls in the middle section: seafood hotpot, clay pot rice, stir-fried clams in black bean sauce. Point at what others are eating. Bring HK$300–400 cash for dinner here.
🦪8:00pm
Dinner · Street Stalls
Dai Pai Dong — Temple Street Stalls
Sit down at one of the outdoor stall tables mid-market. Order by pointing or using the laminated picture menus. The move: typhoon shelter crab or clams (if budget allows), stir-fried beef ho fun (flat rice noodles), steamed silken tofu, clay pot rice if they have it, chilled Tsingtao. This is the meal of the trip — it looks like nothing, costs almost nothing, and tastes like the city.
Mid-section of Temple St, Jordan · ~HK$200–350/person including drinks · Cash only
🍺10:00pm
Brewery · Late Night
Moonzen Brewery — Saturday Free-Flow ★★ Brewery Visit
HK's most characterful craft brewery — family-run, beers named after Chinese mythology (Thunder God Pale Ale, Jade Emperor IPA, Moon Goddess Chocolate Stout). The Saturday session runs until 10pm: HK$268/person gets you free-flow self-pour from 8 taps plus a brewery tour. Hidden in an industrial estate in Kwun Tong — take a goods lift to the 2nd floor, red doors on your left. The crowd is local and expat, the vibe is low-key and genuinely cool. You're coming from Temple Street (Jordan MTR) — Kwun Tong is 3 stops on the Kwun Tong Line, ~8 min. The perfect late cap to Day 3.
2A, 2/F New East Sun Building, 18 Shing Yip St, Kwun Tong · Sat 6–10pm (last call 9:30pm) · HK$268/person free-flow · Book via moonzenbrewery.com · Cash / PayMe · No food served — bring snacks or order delivery

Macau — Old Town (Your Real Destination)
🏛10:30am
UNESCO · Architecture
Senado Square + Ruins of St. Paul's ★★
Senado Square is the center of the historic zone — Portuguese mosaic-paved plaza surrounded by pastel colonial buildings. Zero resemblance to anywhere else in Asia. Walk the alleys radiating off it: Rua da Felicidade (Happiness Street, old brothel district, now noodle shops and old-HK storefronts), Rua dos Ervanários. Then up to the Ruins of St. Paul's — the 17th-century Jesuit cathedral facade. Behind it, the Fortaleza do Monte (old fort) gives the best elevated view of the old town. Budget 60–90 minutes to wander this whole area.
Senado Square: Largo do Senado, Macau · Walk from Outer Harbor Pier ~15 min or cab MOP$60
🥐11:30am
Must-Eat · Pastry
Lord Stow's Bakery — Portuguese Egg Tarts ★★ Original
The original Macau Portuguese egg tart — invented by Andrew Stow in 1989. Flaky pastry shell, creamy caramelized custard, slightly charred top. Completely different from the HK-style (which is Cantonese and uses shortcrust). The Macau version is what the Bakehouse in HK was inspired by. Eat them hot, standing outside. Get 3–4 each. The Coloane Village location (a short cab ride, the original) is quieter than the Senado Square branch — either is fine.
Rua do Tassara 1, Coloane Village (original) OR Largo do Senado branch · Opens 7am · ~MOP$12 each
🍗1:00pm
Lunch · Macanese
African Chicken + Macanese Lunch
African chicken (galinha à africana) is the signature Macanese dish — a Portuguese-Mozambican hybrid, the chicken marinated in coconut, chili, and spice, then roasted. It doesn't exist anywhere else in this form. Restaurante Litoral near Senado Square is the standard-bearer — homey, Macanese family recipes, excellent. Order the African chicken, minchi (Macanese ground beef and potato, like a deconstructed shepherd's pie), and a glass of vinho verde. This is the flavor that justifies the day trip.
Restaurante Litoral: 261 Rua do Almirante Sérgio · Or ask at Lord Stow's for current rec · ~MOP$200–300/person
🎰2:30pm
Optional · Casinos
The Venetian or Grand Lisboa — Brief Wander
You don't need to gamble to visit. The Venetian Macau is 3x the size of the Las Vegas Venetian — the sheer scale is absurd. The Grand Lisboa is genuinely wild architecturally — a golden lotus flower skyscraper. Worth 30 min of walking through the main floor for the spectacle. The casino strip (Cotai) is a ~10 min cab from the old town. Optional — skip if you want more time in the historic zone.
The Venetian: Cotai Strip · Grand Lisboa: Avenida de Lisboa, central Macau · Free to enter

Evening — The Anchor Dinner
🦆7:30pm
Dinner · Fine Dining · Peking Duck
Hutong ★★ Peking Duck Book 4–6 Weeks
The honeymoon dinner. You're back from Macau, cleaned up, and ready for the best table in Kowloon. 28th floor, One Peking Road, TST — floor-to-ceiling windows with the full HK Island skyline lit up across the harbor. Northern Chinese cuisine: the whole Peking duck is carved tableside over three courses — crispy skin with pancakes and hoisin first, meat stir-fried second, bones made into a soup third. Order the Crispy De-boned Lamb Ribs as a starter. Day 4 is the right slot: you've had two street evenings already (Wan Chai stalls Day 2, Temple Street Day 3), Macau Portuguese lunch today — the fine dining lands here with proper weight.
28/F, One Peking Rd, Tsim Sha Tsui · hutong.com.hk · Book 4–6 weeks ahead · ~HK$800–1,200/person · Mention honeymoon
Cross to Kowloon via Star Ferry from Central Pier (7 min, HK$3.40) — you arrive on the TST waterfront with the skyline behind you, walk straight to One Peking Rd. The crossing is part of the evening.
🌃10:00pm
Night Cap · Optional
Ozone — World's Highest Bar (Optional) or Star Ferry Back
Ozone is the world's highest bar (118th floor, ICC Tower, West Kowloon). Walk-in, dress code. The cocktails are fine, the view is the point — at night you look down on all of HK Island. Optional extra if you want to extend the evening. Or take the Star Ferry back from TST Pier to Central (last sailing ~11:30pm) — 7 min across the harbor at night, skyline receding behind you. The perfect end to the best evening of the trip.

18 Grams — Specialty Coffee
Your third specialty roaster in HK. The Central branch is the natural choice — walkable from the hotel downhill, or one MTR stop. Single-origin filter, precise, unpretentious. Start here before heading into the streets.

Breakfast · Classic
Mak's Noodles ★ Must
The best wonton noodle soup in Hong Kong — and one of the most specific food experiences on the trip. The wontons are smaller than any you've had before, made with a fine shrimp paste filling, in a delicate clear broth. The noodles are springy and thin. A bowl is HK$50. The magic is in the broth. Order the wonton noodle soup. Nothing else, just that. Eat it all, including the broth. This is a Michelin Bib Gourmand — it looks like a lunch counter and is one of the city's treasured institutions.
77 Wellington St, Central · Opens 11am (confirm hours) · ~HK$50–70/person · Walk-in · No frills

Tai Kwun — Former Central Police Station ★
The former Central Police Station, magistracy, and Victoria Prison — now a heritage and arts center. The restoration by Herzog & de Meuron (same architects as M+) is immaculate. The stone colonial buildings are among the best-preserved in HK. Walk the prison yard, look into the cells. Free to enter most areas. The courtyard has a good coffee setup if you need one. Architecture · Oddity
Yick Cheong Building (Monster Building) — Quarry Bay
Five massive housing blocks from the 1960s arranged in a U-shape — when you stand in the inner courtyard and look straight up, you get one of the most vertiginous, cinematic urban views anywhere in Asia. Used in Transformers 4 and countless Hong Kong films. This is not a tourist attraction — people live here. Walk in, stand in the middle, look up. Then leave. 20 minutes, completely worth it.

Open Questions + Decisions Still Needed

Things That Still Need a Decision

  • Char Siu Restaurant vs Stall — Chukfo Taipan is the recommendation for dedicated char siu (whole roasted BBQ pork, not just pork on rice). Fits Day 5 late night or a Wan Chai lunch. If you want it as a standalone experience rather than a stall grab, slot it on Day 2 lunch instead of the market stalls.
  • Kwan Kee Claypot Rice — On your spreadsheet. Clay pot rice (claypot with a charred crust) is a classic HK winter dish but runs year-round at Kwan Kee. Fits Day 3 or Day 5 as an afternoon/early dinner detour. Worth it if you want to check it off.
  • Ding Ding Tram — You flagged it. The old double-decker electric tram runs the length of HK Island for HK$3. Best at night through Wan Chai and Causeway Bay — hop on, ride for 20 min, hop off. Fits into Day 2 or Day 5 evening as a transport-as-experience move. Not a dedicated stop, just take it instead of a cab.
  • Traditional Foot Massage — On your spreadsheet. Wan Chai and Mong Kok have excellent reflexology parlors — ~HK$250 for 45 min. Good on Day 3 afternoon after all the walking, before M+. No booking needed at most places.
  • Typhoon Shelter Seafood — A unique HK experience (floating cooking boats in Causeway Bay typhoon shelter) — not in the current itinerary. Could replace the Ho Lee Fook dinner on Day 1 for something more local, or add as a Day 3 late dinner alternative. More chaotic and stall-forward than Ho Lee Fook but very HK.
  • Star Ferry — Take It Every Time — Not a one-time experience. The Star Ferry should be your default crossing whenever you have 7 extra minutes. Every ride across the harbor is different. MTR is faster; Star Ferry is better.
